## Named answer: (b) Kafka pipeline cap

Quantitative justification (3 independent supports):
- Producer aggregate record_send_rate = 407,602/s vs consumer aggregate
  records_consumed_rate = 257,420/s. Producer is 1.58x faster than
  consumer can drain, so the broker / consumer-fetch is the bottleneck.
- Producer accumulator queue time = 1,766 ms (records sit in the
  accumulator) vs broker request_latency_avg = 9.65 ms (actual network
  roundtrip). 177x ratio confirms records are queued in the producer,
  waiting on broker write-rate, not on network.
- VT callback body = 6.4 us out of 2.7-3.8 s vt_produce_ack_wait. Only
  1/500,000th is JVM-internal callback dispatch, so callback starvation
  hypothesis (c) is rejected.

Leader thread itself is ~14x over-provisioned at this throughput cap:
per-record wall 4,307 ns x 8 pool workers = 1.86M ops/s leader headroom
vs 132k ops/s actual.

## Production-priority implications

(b) means the cap is a **benchmark artifact** — the in-process broker
is the limiter, not anything in Venice's AA leader code path. Production
deployment with real Kafka brokers on dedicated hardware would not see
this 132k cap. Production refactor priority is therefore LOW for this
specific finding; the previously-identified `newPutSupersedesAll`
refactor (Phase 5) remains the high-priority production action.
